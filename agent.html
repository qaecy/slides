Querying the Knowledge Graph

A playbook for exploring AEC project data with the agent

sits on top of a Knowledge Graph built from your project's documents, BIM models, contracts, drawings and more

Rather than searching free text, the agent navigates relationships between typed entities — people, organizations, building elements, materials, obligations, drawings

Every fact in the graph is a typed triple:
Subject — predicate — Object

The agent can answer questions by walking these relationships — across document types, disciplines and systems

The richer the graph, the richer the answers

Communities in the graph

The knowledge graph naturally organises into clusters of tightly connected predicates

  • 📐 Drawings — sheets, viewports, disciplines, scales, authors
  • 📄 Contracts — obligations, conditions, responsible parties
  • 🧱 Building Elements ↔ Materials — the tightest single edge
  • 🏢 Parties & Places — people, organizations, locations
  • 🗓️ Projects & Activities — phases, sequences, resources

Where to start

Run /graph-overview to get a profile of your project graph

Then run /relation-analysis to see which communities are dense in this specific project

Use those results to pick which questions are worth asking

Drawings

Usually the largest community by volume

Spine: DrawingSheet → DrawingEntity → DesignMetadata
Authorship and provenance fan out to Person, Organization, Date, Discipline, Scale

D1 "Who are the most prolific drawing authors, and who approves the most sheets?"
Surfaces approval bottlenecks when approval count ≪ creation count
D2 "Which disciplines tend to appear together on the same drawing sheet?"
Cross-discipline coordination heatmap
D3 "Are there sheets where the approval date predates the creation date, or where the gap is unusually long?"
Workflow timing + data quality
D4 "Which drawings are missing a creation date, author, or file reference?"
Extraction-quality audit
D5 "Which file references are cited by the most drawings?"
Hub deliverables: master PDFs, source CAD files
D6 "How is drawing coverage distributed across disciplines and scales?"
Coverage check across MEP / Arch / Struct at different scales

Contracts

Typically the cleanest sub-graph — typed predicates are tightly scoped

Best place to walk a full multi-hop chain in a single query

A single query can tell the complete legal story of a contract

  • What are its obligations?
  • Under what conditions does each obligation apply?
  • Who is responsible for each obligation?
C1 "For each contract, what are the obligations, conditions, and who is responsible?"
3-hop join — one query = complete legal story
C2 "Which organizations appear in the most contracts, and in what roles?"
Party-influence ranking
C3 "Which contracts have a signature date but are missing a start or end date?"
Lifecycle completeness audit
C4 "Which built assets are referenced across multiple contracts?"
Cross-contract asset exposure
C5 "When obligations are assigned, are responsible parties more often people or organizations?"
Delegation vs. corporate responsibility pattern

Building Elements ↔ Materials

Usually the tightest single edge in the graph

elementHasMaterial exclusively connects BuildingElement subtypes — Wall, Slab, Window — to Material

BIM objects become typed entity mentions in the graph

M1 "What are walls made of? What about slabs, windows?"
Definitive answers — the cleanest cut in the graph
M2 "Which materials are used across multiple element subtypes?"
Universal materials vs. subtype-specific ones
M3 "Which building elements have no material assigned?"
BIM-side extraction completeness
M4 "Which materials are reused across the most distinct elements?"
Material catalogue centrality

Parties & Places

The connective tissue of the graph

Person → Organization → Location / Address / City / Email
Connects everything else

Entity resolution links mentions of the same person or organization across all documents

P1 "Who works for which organization, and where is that organization located?"
Walks the social-graph backbone in 2 hops
P2 "Which organizations share an address — subsidiaries, joint ventures, shared offices?"
Corporate structure patterns
P3 "Which people are associated with more than one organization?"
Multi-org individuals: consultants, secondments
P4 "Which organizations are the most connected in the graph overall?"
Hub parties — often appear in both contracts AND drawings

Projects & Activities

Typically the sparsest community — confirm density with /relation-analysis before deep-diving

A1 "What phases does this project have, and where do they happen?"
A2 "What equipment and materials does each activity require?"
A3 "Which activities touch which building elements, and what are those elements made of?"
Narratively rich when it returns rows
A4 "What is the sequence of activities — which precede which?"
Transitive walk over precedesActivity

Cross-community joins

These bridge communities — strongest narrative payoff per query

The most interesting stories live at the intersection of communities

"Which organizations appear both as drawing authors and as contract parties?"

Intersects Drawings ↔ Contracts — usually a short list of key firms

X1 "Which organizations appear both as drawing authors and as contract parties?"
X2 "Do built assets named in contracts overlap with the building elements drawn in design documents?"
Connects legal ↔ design layers
X3 "Which people hold a contract role AND have authored drawings?"
Key individuals across legal / design
X4 "Are there contracts whose obligations name a person who also appears as a drawing approver?"
Full accountability chain across both domains

Canonical entities are the anchors that make cross-community joins possible

Semantic search

When the question is fuzzy, spatial, or doesn't fit the structural schema

Use /search when you need full-text reasoning across document content

Follow up with /query-graph to resolve hits to canonical entities

  • "Which documents discuss fire-safety obligations?"
  • "Are there drawings related to the basement or foundation?"
  • "Is there anything in the documents about delays or schedule risk?"
  • "Where is Rockwool mentioned, and how do those mentions map to canonical Material entities?"

Data quality audits

The graph makes gaps visible — and gaps are actionable

Gap queries identify missing metadata across any document type

  • "Which drawings are missing a creation date or author?"
  • "Which contracts have a signature date but no end date?"
  • "Which building elements have no material assigned?"
  • "Which organizations have no email address on record?"

Use /list-predicates to audit ontology coverage

Predicates that dominate vs. those in the long tail tell you where extraction is working well

Questions about the graph itself

S1 "What are the most-connected canonical entities in this project?"
/graph-overview returns this directly
S2 "Which documents have contributed the most typed relations, and to which communities?"
Which documents are most graph-productive
S3 "Which predicates dominate the graph, and which sit in the long tail?"
/list-predicates

Rules of thumb

  • For a clean multi-hop story → start with Contracts
  • For volume and breadth → start with Drawings
  • For a precise BIM answer → Elements ↔ Materials
  • For "who matters?" → Cross-community joins
  • For data quality → the gap queries
  • For fuzzy exploration → /search first, then /query-graph

Always start a new project with:

  1. /graph-overview
  2. /relation-analysis

Use those results to decide which communities are worth exploring in this project
